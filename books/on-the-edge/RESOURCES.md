# On the Edge Resources

## Knowledge

### Cross-cutting
- *On the Edge: The Art of Risking Everything* (Nate Silver, Penguin Press, 2024) — primary, the user's own copy. Full text and its 250+ term glossary, "How to Speak Riverian," read in full for grounding — not summarized from reviews. Cite by chapter (Introduction; Part 1: Optimization, Perception, Consumption, Competition; Halftime: Inspiration; Part 2: Acceleration, Illusion, Quantification, Miscalculation, Termination, Foundation) since there's no free public text.
- [Welcome to the River](https://www.natesilver.net/p/welcome-to-the-river) — Nate Silver, Silver Bulletin (Substack), free, primary. The essay-length version of the book's central taxonomy.
- [One year later, is the River winning?](https://www.natesilver.net/p/one-year-later-is-the-river-winning) — Silver's own follow-up essay, free, primary.

### The River, the Village, and decoupling
- Introduction (Chapter 0) — the taxonomy itself, the Sarah Constantin definition of decoupling, and Silver's own steelman critiques in both directions ("The River's Steelman Critique of the Village" and "The Village's Steelman Critique of the River" — he writes both sides).

### Expected value, optionality, and edge
- Chapter 1 (Optimization) — EV, edge, optionality (the beach/picnic example), Nash equilibrium vs. exploitative play (the Verlander/Betts pitch-guessing example).

### Risk of ruin, the Kelly criterion, and bankroll
- Chapter 8 (Miscalculation) — the Kelly criterion explained through Sam Bankman-Fried's own stated rejection of it ("he thought it was for wimps"), including Silver's own 5,000-simulated-season comparison of full Kelly vs. 5x Kelly.
- Chapter ∞ (Termination) — Kelly criterion referenced again re: Sam Altman and existential stakes.

### Results-oriented thinking, survivorship bias
- Chapter 2 (Perception) — Silver's own admission: "this book potentially suffers from survivorship bias because it focuses more on successful risk-takers than failed ones."
- Chapter 5 (Acceleration) — section literally titled "Skill, Survivorship Bias, and Right-Skewed Outcomes; or, Did Elon Musk Just Get Lucky?"

### Calibration, the outside view, forecasting
- Chapter 5 (Acceleration) — Tetlock's fox/hedgehog framework applied to VCs and founders; interview material with Chamath Palihapitiya.
- Chapter 7 (Quantification) — connects Tetlock's forecasting-accountability point to effective altruism and Yudkowsky's p(doom).

### Habits of successful risk-takers
- Chapter 13 (Inspiration / "Halftime") — a set of named habits (prepared, selectively attentive, adaptable, good estimators/Bayesian, non-conformist, "conscientiously contrarian," not money-driven), each argued through a named interview subject.

### Where expected-value logic breaks
- Chapter 7 (Quantification) — the Repugnant Conclusion (Derek Parfit) in detail, plus philosopher Lara Buchak's critique of utilitarianism's reliance on outlier/infinite cases.
- Chapter 8 (Miscalculation) — Sam Bankman-Fried's coin-flip-with-the-universe thought experiment and the St. Petersburg paradox.

### Effective altruism and longtermism
- Chapter 7 (Quantification) — Peter Singer's drowning-child parable and the principle of impartiality, longtermism (Will MacAskill), and Silver's own counter-thought-experiment (trading ten loved ones for eleven strangers).

### Existential risk and AI
- Chapter ∞ (Termination) — the orthogonality thesis and instrumental convergence (Bostrom, via Yudkowsky), the paper-clip maximizer thought experiment, AI takeoff speed.

### The Village's steelman critique of the River
- Introduction (Chapter 0), the section explicitly titled "The Village's Steelman Critique of the River" — Silver's own strongest case against his book's central thesis.

## Wisdom (Communities)
- [LessWrong](https://www.lesswrong.com/) — verified active as of 2026 (checked directly, not assumed). The actual rationalist community the book profiles (Yudkowsky, the "rat" archetype in the glossary).
- [EA Forum](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/) — verified active as of 2026. The actual effective-altruism community the book profiles (MacAskill, longtermism, the drowning-child material).
- Both are the real-world "River" communities this book is reporting on, not a generic recommendation — testing ideas from lessons 11-13 against people who actually hold these views is closer to primary engagement than reading about them secondhand.
