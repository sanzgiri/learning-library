Cognitive biases in risk perception

On the Edge — Chapter 2, Perception

A coin lands heads five times running. Some people feel sure tails is "due." Others feel sure heads is "hot." Both feelings are confident, opposite, and equally wrong — the coin has no memory either way.

The idea

The gambler's fallacy is the belief that random outcomes must "balance out" soon (tails is due); the hot hand fallacy is its mirror image (heads is hot, keep betting heads). Both misread pure randomness as a pattern. Prospect theory (Kahneman and Tversky) describes a related, well-documented asymmetry: people are risk-averse when facing the loss of something they already have, often preferring a guaranteed $300 to a coin flip that pays $1,000 or costs $100 — even though the flip has a higher EV. Anchoring bias rounds out the set: being "weighted down by information you learn early in your decision-making process" and under-adjusting as better information arrives.

None of these are exotic — they're the default operating mode of a normally functioning human brain. What separates a "Riverian" read on risk from a "normal" one isn't the absence of these instincts, it's building habits and checks that catch them before they turn into a decision. Chapter 2's title, "Perception," is the book's own admission that most of the fight against bad risk-taking happens before any math gets involved — in how the situation felt in the first place.

Practice

1. Catch yourself, this week, in a live instance of one of these four biases (gambler's fallacy, hot hand, prospect-theory loss aversion, or anchoring) — not a hypothetical, an actual decision you made or were tempted to make. Which one was it, and what did the "correct" EV-based read of the same situation look like?

2. Prospect theory says most people would take a guaranteed $300 over a coin flip worth $1,000 or –$100, even though the flip is higher EV. Would you? Is preferring the safe $300 actually irrational, or is there a legitimate reason — beyond raw EV — that a real person might prefer it?

Something to sit with

These biases evolved for reasons — loss aversion probably kept our ancestors alive more often than it got them killed. If a bias was adaptive across most of human history, is "correcting" it toward pure EV-maximization actually an improvement for an individual life, or does it just make you better at a narrow class of modern problems (gambling, trading, forecasting) at some other cost?
