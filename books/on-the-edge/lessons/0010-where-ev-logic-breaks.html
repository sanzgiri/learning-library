LEVEL 10~14 MIN

Where expected-value logic breaks

On the Edge — Chapters 7 & 8

Compare two worlds: one with 8 billion people living rich, flourishing lives; another with an unimaginably vast number of people whose lives are barely worth living at all — say, eating one bad meal before dying. Strict utilitarian math says the second world is better, because an unimaginably large number times a tiny positive value still beats a large number times a big one. Something has clearly gone wrong, and it's worth being precise about what.

The idea

That's philosopher Derek Parfit's Repugnant Conclusion, and Silver uses it to press on the limits of the EV-maximizing worldview this whole course has been building. Princeton philosopher Lara Buchak, whom Silver interviews, argues the actual test of a moral theory shouldn't be whether it handles infinite edge cases elegantly — it should be whether it handles "should I bring my umbrella today"-level everyday decisions well. If a theory only works by biting extreme, repugnant bullets, "then we probably shouldn't trust it."

Pascal's Mugging sharpens the same worry into a single move: a mugger threatens a tiny, made-up probability of catastrophe unless you pay him — and because he's invented an arbitrarily large claimed harm, naive expected-value math can be forced to recommend paying, no matter how absurd the threat. Silver connects this directly back to Sam Bankman-Fried's own "coin flip with the universe" reasoning from lesson 3: if you'll take a +EV bet on total annihilation an unlimited number of times because the math says to, you've been mugged by your own formula. In philosophical terms, this is the St. Petersburg paradox again — a bet with literally infinite expected value that guarantees ruin if repeated, because the astronomical payoff lives in a vanishingly unlikely branch that "basically means zero chance" in practice.

None of this means expected value is wrong as a tool. It means EV-maximization has edge cases — infinities, tiny-probability catastrophic claims, aggregation across huge populations — where the math technically outputs an answer that should make you suspicious of the math, not confident in the answer.

Practice

1. Construct your own small-scale version of Pascal's Mugging — a claim with a tiny, hard-to-verify probability and an enormous stated stake, of the kind you actually encounter (a fear-based sales pitch, an alarmist argument, a "what if" objection to a plan). What's the actual tell that distinguishes it from a real, well-grounded tail risk?

2. Buchak's test is: does the theory work for everyday decisions, not just extreme ones? Apply that test to one piece of EV-style advice you've absorbed from this course so far. Does it hold up on an ordinary Tuesday, or only in the dramatic examples used to teach it?

Something to sit with

Silver's own answer to the Repugnant Conclusion is close to "who the fuck cares — we have 8 billion people to worry about, let's not get distracted by trolley problems." Is that a legitimate philosophical position (some edge cases just aren't worth optimizing for), or is it a way of quietly abandoning the very framework (rigorous EV reasoning) the rest of the book asks you to trust?

Talk it through with your tutor below — they're listening for whether your Pascal's Mugging example actually has the right shape (tiny unverifiable probability, huge claimed stakes), not just any scary argument.
