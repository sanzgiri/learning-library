LEVEL 11~14 MIN

Effective altruism & longtermism

On the Edge — Chapter 7, Quantification

Philosopher Peter Singer asks you to imagine a child drowning in a shallow pond. Saving her means ruining your suit. You'd do it without a second thought — her life is obviously worth more than a suit. Singer's actual point isn't about the pond. It's about what that easy answer commits you to next.

The idea

If ruining an $800 suit to save a nearby child is obviously right, Singer argues, then donating that same $800 to save a child you'll never meet, thousands of miles away, is morally identical — you're just as capable of saving a life either way, and distance shouldn't change the math. That's the principle of impartiality: "we ought to regard people further removed from us… as being just as morally worthy" as those close to us. Effective altruism (EA) — founded by Will MacAskill and others — takes this and asks the natural next question: if you accept impartiality, shouldn't you also find the intervention that saves the most lives per dollar, rather than the one that feels good to fund? Longtermism pushes the same impartiality further still, in time rather than space: MacAskill's version holds that a person born in the year 3024 has the same moral weight as an infant born today — a claim Silver notes is "controversial even among EAs."

Silver takes the parable seriously enough to construct his own counter-thought-experiment: would you euthanize the ten people you love most to save eleven random strangers? Almost everyone says no — even people who accept the drowning-child logic completely. His conclusion: "we should probably be less selfish, but we have to be partially partial." Full impartiality, taken all the way, produces conclusions that even its own advocates flinch from.

Practice

1. Where in your own giving, time, or attention do you already act on something like the impartiality principle — treating a stranger's need as comparably real to a friend's? Where do you explicitly not, and can you defend that boundary, or is it just habit?

2. Take Silver's ten-for-eleven thought experiment seriously for a moment. If you (like most people) say no, what does that "no" actually reveal about how much impartiality you really believe in, versus how much you say you believe in?

Something to sit with

Longtermism asks you to weight a person centuries from now as heavily as someone alive today. That's the same impartiality move Singer's drowning-child parable already got you to accept for distance — just applied to time instead of geography. If you accepted the first move, is there a principled reason to reject the second, or does consistency require you to take longtermism at least as seriously as you take ordinary charity?
