LEVEL 04~12 MIN

Results-oriented thinking vs. process

On the Edge — Chapters 2 & 5

Every profile of a great poker player or a legendary founder is, structurally, a profile of someone who won. The ones who made the identical decision and lost are not being interviewed for the book.

The idea

Silver names the failure mode directly: results-oriented thinking is "judging the wisdom of an action by the result… disregarding the role of luck and the process behind the action." He argues Riverians are trained to resist this by design — professional poker culture explicitly rewards good process even after a bad result, and punishes lucky wins built on bad decisions — while "results-oriented thinking predominates outside the River."

Chapter 5 gives the sharpest version of the question, in a section literally titled "Skill, Survivorship Bias, and Right-Skewed Outcomes; or, Did Elon Musk Just Get Lucky?" And Silver doesn't spare his own book: in Chapter 2 he writes plainly, "this book potentially suffers from survivorship bias because it focuses more on successful risk-takers than failed ones who have faded into obscurity." Survivorship bias is exactly that — your sample is contaminated because failure is more likely to disappear from view than success is.

Put the two together and you get a genuine bind, not a clean answer: you can't fully separate skill from luck by looking at outcomes alone, but you also can't just declare every successful person "probably lucky" and move on — some of them really do have an edge. The discipline the book asks for is judging the decision at the time it was made, with the information available then, rather than working backward from how it turned out.

Practice

1. Pick one person (yourself or someone else) whose success you've been tempted to credit entirely to skill. Try to actually reconstruct the decision process at the time — not the outcome — and ask whether it would have looked equally sound in the world where it failed.

2. Silver admits his own book suffers from survivorship bias. Does that admission actually fix the problem, or is naming a bias without correcting for it just a more sophisticated way of doing the same thing? What would an actual correction look like for a book like this?

Something to sit with

If judging process instead of results is right in principle, it's also exploitable — anyone can claim "the process was sound, I just got unlucky" after any failure, with no way to check. What would it actually take to hold someone accountable for their process rather than their luck, without that standard becoming an excuse for never being wrong?

Talk it through with your tutor below — they're listening for whether you reconstructed a real decision process at the time it was made, not just relabeled an outcome as "probably luck" or "probably skill."
