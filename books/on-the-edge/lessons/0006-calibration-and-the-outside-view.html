LEVEL 06~14 MIN

Calibration & the outside view

On the Edge — Chapter 5, Acceleration

"The fox knows many things, but the hedgehog knows one big thing." Nate Silver borrows this line — via political scientist Philip Tetlock — to explain why some forecasters are reliably better than others, and it isn't the ones with the one big theory.

The idea

Tetlock's long-running study of expert political forecasters found most of them were bad at it — but the ones with a cluster of fox-like traits (incrementalist, willing to hold many small, updatable views) reliably outperformed hedgehogs (committed to one big, ideologically consistent theory of the case). Calibration is the actual measurable standard behind this: if you say a class of events will happen 20% of the time, they should happen close to 20% of the time across many such forecasts — not more, not less.

The outside view is the fox's characteristic move: "a prediction made without domain knowledge or inside information, instead calculated using broadly applicable heuristics or long-term base rates," which "often proves more accurate than the inside view" despite seeming naive. Silver applies this directly to venture capital — VCs hearing hundreds of pitches across many sectors are structurally foxes, forced to know "a little about a lot," which is exactly the trait Tetlock's research says predicts good forecasting. Founders, by contrast, are almost definitionally hedgehogs — you don't build one company for a decade by holding your commitment loosely — which is why Silver argues founders and VCs need each other: one supplies conviction, the other supplies calibration.

Practice

1. Are you, in your current main pursuit, playing the fox or the hedgehog? Is that the right role for what you're actually trying to do, per Silver's founder/VC distinction — or have you defaulted into a role that doesn't fit the situation?

2. Take one forecast you've made recently with some confidence (about a person, a market, an outcome). What would "calibration" actually require you to do to check whether that confidence was justified — not just this once, but across a track record?

Something to sit with

Foxes forecast better, per Tetlock's data — but Silver also notes hedgehogs "may be less reliable at forecasting and incremental decision-making, but are more natural leaders since their resolve may compel others to join them." If hedgehogs are worse forecasters but better leaders, what does a society or organization actually want more of — and is there a way to get the fox's accuracy and the hedgehog's ability to move people, or is that a real trade-off you can't escape?

Talk it through with your tutor below — they're listening for whether you've applied fox/hedgehog and calibration to a real situation of yours, not just recited Tetlock's finding.
