Expected value & edge

On the Edge — Chapter 1, Optimization

A coin flip pays you $110 if it lands heads and costs you $100 if it lands tails. Should you take it? Most people's gut says "it's basically a coin flip, so it doesn't matter much." A Riverian's gut does arithmetic instead.

The idea

Expected value (EV) is "the result you expect to wind up with on average after many 'spins of the wheel,' given your knowledge of the odds." For that coin flip: 50% × $110 + 50% × (–$100) = +$5. Positive EV, even though any single flip is close to a coin toss. Having an edge means being reliably +EV over the long run — not winning this particular bet, but being the kind of player who comes out ahead if you ran the same decision a thousand times.

Silver's own illustration of the deeper move is a rained-out picnic: should you drive to the beach anyway on a 30% chance the weather clears? If a beach picnic is worth 10, a restaurant near the beach is worth 5, and a restaurant in town is worth 6, "going to the beach is the higher EV option if there's a 30 percent chance of rain" — because heading to the beach preserves the chance of the best outcome without fully giving up a decent fallback. That's optionality smuggled inside an EV calculation: the value isn't just in the odds, it's in keeping a good branch of the decision tree open.

The trap is treating "I calculated an EV" as the end of the analysis. An EV is only as good as the probability estimate feeding it — and, as later lessons in this course will press on, a positive EV bet can still be a terrible idea if a bad outcome would wipe you out before the "long run" ever arrives.

Practice

1. Take one recurring decision in your life that you currently make on gut feel (whether to take a meeting, whether to say yes to a favor, whether to try a new vendor). Rough out an actual EV for it — even crude numbers — the way Silver does with the picnic.

2. Name one decision where you had "an edge" in Silver's sense — not a one-time win, but a repeatable reason to expect to come out ahead over many tries. How would you actually verify that the edge was real, rather than a lucky run?

Something to sit with

EV thinking assumes you get to see "the long run" — that you'll take something like this bet many times. Some of life's biggest decisions (which career, which partner, whether to have a child) you only get to make once or a handful of times. Does expected value even mean anything for a decision you can't repeat? What should replace it, if anything?

Talk it through with your tutor below — they're listening for whether you built a real, if rough, EV calculation rather than just restating that "some decisions are worth it on average."
