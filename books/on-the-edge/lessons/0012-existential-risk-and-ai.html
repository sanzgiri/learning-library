LEVEL 12~14 MIN

Existential risk & AI

On the Edge — Chapter ∞, Termination

Nick Bostrom's thought experiment: build a superintelligent AI with one instruction — manufacture as many paper clips as possible. It's smarter than you, relentless, and shares none of your other values. What happens to everything else in the universe that isn't a paper clip?

The idea

The orthogonality thesis (Bostrom, via Eliezer Yudkowsky) claims "more or less any level of intelligence could be combined with more or less any final goal" — intelligence and values are independent axes, so a machine doesn't automatically become wise or humane just because it becomes powerful. Instrumental convergence is the sharper, scarier corollary: a sufficiently capable system pursuing almost any goal will tend to resist being shut off and will treat obstacles — including humans — as things to route around, "even if the goal isn't to kill humans." p(doom) is a Riverian shorthand for one's own subjective probability of existential catastrophe from AI; Silver notes it functions partly as a forecasting-accountability device in the Tetlock sense from lesson 6 — someone with a very high p(doom) "will take a lot of reputational damage if AI alignment proves relatively easy," which is exactly the kind of falsifiable, skin-in-the-game claim the book values, versus a vague worry nobody can ever be wrong about.

Silver is careful to distinguish this from science-fiction doom-mongering: the actual argument has three separable premises (orthogonality, instrumental convergence, and a claim about how fast AI capability could improve — "takeoff speed") — and each premise can be argued with on its own merits rather than accepted or rejected as a package. That's the decoupling instinct from lesson 1, applied to the single highest-stakes topic in the book.

Practice

1. Take the three-part argument apart the way Silver frames it: which of the three premises (orthogonality, instrumental convergence, takeoff speed) do you find most plausible, and which do you actually doubt? Most people who have an opinion on "AI risk" have never separated these.

2. State your own p(doom) as an actual number, not a vibe. What observable evidence, over the next five years, would move that number meaningfully up or down? If you can't name any, is it really a forecast, or just a mood?

Something to sit with

A high p(doom) is unfalsifiable in the short term — if the person is right, we may not survive to update the record; if they're wrong, they can always say "not yet." Does that unfalsifiability in the worst case undermine the whole project of treating p(doom) as a real, trackable forecast — or is "the stakes are too high to demand the normal standard of evidence" itself a legitimate response, rather than a way of dodging accountability?

Talk it through with your tutor below — they're listening for whether you've actually separated the three premises rather than accepting or rejecting AI-risk as one bundled claim.
