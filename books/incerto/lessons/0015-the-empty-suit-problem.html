LEVEL 15~12 MIN

The empty-suit problem

Fooled by Randomness / Incerto Glossary

Some professions have a real, checkable track record: a surgeon's patients either survive or don't, a bridge engineer's bridges either stand or fall. Other professions produce confident, well-dressed, articulate opinions with no comparable record ever being checked against outcomes — and get treated as expertise anyway.

The idea

Taleb's term for this, deliberately unflattering, is the empty-suit problem.

"Empty-suit problem (or 'expert problem'): Some professionals have no differential abilities from the rest of the population, but for some reason, and against their empirical records, are believed to be experts: clinical psychologists, academic economists, risk 'experts,' statisticians, political analysts, financial 'experts,' military analysts, CEOs, et cetera. They dress up their expertise in beautiful language, jargon, mathematics, and often wear expensive suits." Taleb, Incerto Glossary

Notice this is distinct from the Intellectual Yet Idiot (lesson 7). The IYI critique is about accountability — confident advice with no personal cost when it's wrong. The empty-suit problem is narrower and more testable: it's about whether the profession's confident-sounding output has ever actually beaten chance, regardless of who bears the cost. A field can have an empty-suit problem even where practitioners genuinely believe they're helping and even bear some cost themselves — the defect is purely in the gap between claimed skill and demonstrated skill.

The tell, per Taleb, isn't the content of the claims — it's the presentation. Jargon, formalism, and expensive signaling substitute for the one thing that would actually settle the question: a checkable record, compared against people with no training at all.

Practice

1. Pick one professional category from Taleb's list (or a similar one you know well). What would an honest, checkable track record for that profession actually look like — and has anyone published one?

2. Name one signal you personally use to grant someone "expert" status (vocabulary, credentials, confidence, production values). How well does that signal actually correlate with a checkable track record, as far as you know?

Something to sit with

Some fields genuinely can't produce clean track records — the world doesn't hand out enough repeated, comparable trials (geopolitics, long-range macroeconomics). Does the empty-suit critique still apply to those fields, or does it only bite where a track record was possible and just wasn't kept? What should confidence look like in a field where the record genuinely can't exist?

Talk it through with your tutor below. Your tutor is listening for whether you've actually proposed a testable track record, not just picked a profession you're already skeptical of.
