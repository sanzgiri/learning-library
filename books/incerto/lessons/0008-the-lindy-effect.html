The Lindy effect

Antifragile / "An Expert Called Lindy"

Actors used to gather at Lindy's Delicatessen in Manhattan and compare notes on Broadway shows. They noticed something: a show that had already run for a hundred days tended to run for about a hundred more. A show that had run for two hundred days tended to run for another two hundred. The longer a show had survived, the longer its remaining life expectancy — the opposite of how a person ages.

The idea

Taleb generalizes this into a heuristic for anything non-perishable — ideas, technologies, books, institutions, habits of thought: the longer something has already survived, the longer you should expect it to keep surviving. Age itself is evidence of fitness, because surviving means it has already been tested against a long, uncontrolled series of conditions that no lab experiment could replicate.

"If a book has been in print for forty years, I can expect it to be in print for another forty years. But… if it survives another decade, then it will be expected to be in print another fifty years." Taleb, "An Expert Called Lindy"

This connects directly back to antifragility: the Lindy effect is what antifragility looks like from the outside, over time, when you can't directly observe a system's internal response to stress. You can't always tell whether something is antifragile by inspecting it once — but if it's been repeatedly exposed to disorder and is still here, that survival is itself the evidence. It's also why Taleb is suspicious of "new and improved": a new idea hasn't yet been tested by time, no matter how good it looks on paper.

The heuristic has a limit, though — it applies to things without a fixed, mortal lifespan. A hundred-year-old book benefits from Lindy; a hundred-year-old human does not, because a person has a biological expiration the way a book, a company, or an idea does not.

Practice

1. Name one idea, tool, or institution you're currently drawn to because it's new. Apply Lindy: how long has anything like it actually existed, and what would you predict for its remaining lifespan on that basis alone?

2. Now name one old idea or practice you've been tempted to discard as "outdated." What does its age actually tell you, according to Lindy — and separately, what evidence (beyond age) would you need to decide it's genuinely obsolete rather than just unfashionable?

Something to sit with

Lindy is a heuristic, not a law — it can be wrong, and "it's survived so far" is exactly the turkey's mistake from an earlier lesson if you apply it carelessly. What's the actual difference between "this has survived because it's robust" and "this has survived so far, right up until it doesn't" — and how would you tell which one you're looking at?
