Optionality & convexity

Antifragile

Two people place bets. One needs to be right more than half the time just to break even, because a wrong guess costs as much as a right one pays. The other can be wrong most of the time and still come out ahead, because being wrong costs a little and being right pays a lot. The second person doesn't need a better crystal ball — they need a better-shaped bet.

The idea

That shape is convexity, and having it is what Taleb calls optionality: the right, but not the obligation, to take an upside if conditions turn out favorable, while your downside stays small and known. An option's value comes precisely from that asymmetry — you don't need to predict which way things will go, only to make sure you're structurally positioned to benefit more from good surprises than you're hurt by bad ones.

This is also why tinkering — cheap trial-and-error, lots of small attempts — tends to outperform grand top-down planning in complex, poorly-understood systems: as long as each attempt is convex (small, capped cost of being wrong; open-ended payoff if one happens to work), you can accumulate a few big wins from a pile of small failures. Planning has to be right in advance; tinkering just has to be convex.

Going deeper: why convexity gets missed

If convexity is this valuable, why don't people default to it? Part of the answer is domain dependence — a coming lesson's topic — the tendency to grasp a pattern clearly in one part of life (a trader understands convex payoffs cold) and fail to recognize the identical shape somewhere else (the same trader signs a rigid, all-or-nothing lease, or takes on a job with capped upside and open-ended downside, without noticing the mismatch).

Practice

1. Take one recurring commitment — a subscription, a standing meeting, a side project — and redesign it so it behaves like an option rather than an obligation. What makes the downside bounded in your redesign?

2. What new information, arriving later, would make you glad you kept the option open rather than committing fully now?

3. Find one place in your life where you clearly understand convexity (you'd never take a bet with unlimited downside and capped upside) but where you're currently doing exactly that without noticing. What made it invisible?

Something to sit with

Tinkering beats planning "as long as each attempt is convex" — but most jobs and institutions punish visible small failures even when they're cheap and reversible. What does a culture that punishes small failures end up optimizing for instead, and what does it lose?

Talk it through with your tutor below. Your tutor is listening for whether your redesign is actually convex — bounded downside and open upside — not just "flexible" in a vague sense.
