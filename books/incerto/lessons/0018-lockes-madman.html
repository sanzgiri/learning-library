LEVEL 18~10 MIN

Locke's madman

The Black Swan / Incerto Glossary

A madman, in the old sense John Locke meant, isn't someone who reasons badly — it's someone who reasons perfectly from a premise that's wrong. The logic is airtight from step two onward. The whole edifice just happens to be built on sand.

The idea

"Locke's madman: someone who makes impeccable and rigorous reasoning from faulty premises — such as Paul Samuelson, Robert Merton the minor, and Gerard Debreu — thus producing phony models of uncertainty that make us vulnerable to Black Swans." Taleb, Incerto Glossary

Taleb's target is specific: celebrated economists whose mathematics is genuinely rigorous, applied to a premise — that markets behave like the tame, bell-curve world of Mediocristan — that's false for the fat-tailed reality they're modeling. The rigor is real. It's also exactly what makes the result dangerous: nobody double-checks the premise once the math is beautiful enough, and the beautiful math is precisely what gets taught, published, and trusted.

This is the sharper, adjacent case to the empty-suit problem (lesson 15). An empty suit has no real edge and dresses it up. Locke's madman may have a genuine, rare mathematical gift — the failure isn't a lack of skill, it's applying serious skill to an unexamined premise. Skill at reasoning and correctness of the premise being reasoned from are entirely separate axes, and impressive command of one tells you nothing about the other.

Practice

1. Find one argument you currently find persuasive largely because it's rigorously built (a model, a spreadsheet, a tightly reasoned essay). Separate the two questions: is the reasoning from premise to conclusion actually valid, and is the premise itself true? Which one did you actually check?

2. Name one domain where you'd personally be tempted to trust "the math is really sophisticated" as a proxy for "the underlying assumption is sound." Why does that substitution feel safe, when it isn't?

Something to sit with

Rigor is usually a virtue — we want careful reasoning, not sloppy reasoning. Locke's madman shows rigor can also be camouflage. Is there a way to tell, from the outside, when impressive rigor is illuminating a true premise versus dressing up a false one — or does it always require actually checking the premise yourself, with no shortcut?

Talk it through with your tutor below — they're listening for whether you've actually separated "the logic holds" from "the premise is true," not just named an example of bad math.
