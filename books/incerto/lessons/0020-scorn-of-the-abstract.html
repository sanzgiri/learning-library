LEVEL 20~10 MIN

Scorn of the abstract

Fooled by Randomness / Incerto Glossary

A single named, photographed, particular person in danger can move an entire country to act. The same danger, scaled to a number with no face attached, barely registers. The abstraction isn't a rounding error in our moral reasoning — it's a switch that turns caring on and off.

The idea

"Scorn of the abstract: favoring contextualized thinking over more abstract, though more relevant, matters." Taleb, Incerto Glossary

Taleb's glossary illustrates the bias with a version of the grim line "the death of one child is a tragedy; the death of a million is a statistic" — a line whose exact origin is disputed (it's commonly, and unreliably, attributed to Stalin) but whose observation stands regardless of who said it first: we reason sharply and emotionally about the one concrete case in front of us, and go numb exactly as the numbers get larger and more consequential. The abstract case is more relevant — it's the one with more at stake — and it's precisely the one we're worst equipped to feel anything about.

This connects directly to ergodicity (lesson 12): both lessons are about the gap between what happens to one identifiable case and what happens in aggregate. There, the gap was mathematical (ensemble average vs. time average). Here, it's emotional: the aggregate is real and often the higher-stakes quantity, but it doesn't come with a face, so it doesn't land.

Practice

1. Name one large-scale, high-stakes statistic you know intellectually but don't feel anything about. Now find or imagine one concrete, individual case inside that statistic. Notice the difference in your own reaction — what changed, given that the facts didn't?

2. Where in your own decisions (charitable giving, risk you tolerate for others vs. for one named person, policy opinions) does this switch quietly determine what you actually prioritize, regardless of what you'd say your priorities are if asked directly?

Something to sit with

If we're wired to care about the concrete and go numb toward the abstract, is the right response to distrust that wiring and force yourself toward the numbers — or to accept the wiring and deliberately convert abstractions into concrete cases whenever you need to actually act on them? Which failure mode is worse: being manipulated by a vivid single case, or being paralyzed by an abstraction you can't feel?

Talk it through with your tutor below — they're listening for whether you've found a real case where the switch changed your own behavior, not just described the bias in the abstract (which would be a little ironic).
