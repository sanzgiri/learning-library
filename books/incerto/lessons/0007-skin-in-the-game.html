Skin in the game

Skin in the Game

A medieval general led the charge himself; if the battle plan was bad, he was often the first to find out. A modern advisor can urge a war, a policy, or a trade from a studio or a boardroom and face no personal cost either way. Same category of decision, opposite exposure to being wrong.

The idea

Taleb's core claim is that fairness — and, more practically, the trustworthiness of advice — comes from symmetry: whoever gets the upside of a decision should also bear its downside. When that symmetry breaks — one party collects the reward, another absorbs the risk — you get what he calls the fragility of the whole system, not just an ethical complaint. Advice from someone with no skin in the game isn't necessarily wrong, but it hasn't been tested the way advice from someone who has to live with the result has.

He sharpens this with the idea of the "Intellectual Yet Idiot" — credentialed people confident enough to tell everyone else what to do, eat, say, and think, without ever paying a cost when they're wrong:

"…paternalistic semi-intellectual experts with some Ivy league, Oxford-Cambridge, or similar label-driven education who are telling the rest of us 1) what to do, 2) what to eat, 3) how to speak, 4) how to think… and 5) who to vote for." Taleb, "The Intellectual Yet Idiot" (excerpted chapter, Skin in the Game)

A coming lesson on the minority rule shows the flip side: sometimes a small group with real skin in the game — people who will refuse a bad outcome absolutely, not just prefer against it — ends up controlling the behavior of an entire majority that has no such stake either way.

There's a related asymmetry Taleb calls the Black Swan ethical problem: "owing to the nonrepeatable aspect of the Black Swan, there is an asymmetry between the rewards of those who prevent and those who cure." A doctor who cures a visible disease is celebrated. A public health measure that prevents an epidemic that consequently never happens gets no parade — the disaster it stopped is invisible precisely because it worked. The preventer usually has less skin in the game to gain from succeeding than the curer does, even though prevention is frequently the higher-value act.

Practice

1. Think of someone whose advice you currently give real weight to — a pundit, an analyst, a guru, even a friend. Do they bear any cost if that advice turns out wrong?

2. If not, how much should that change how you weight it — and is there someone giving similar advice who does have skin in the game, whose track record you could check instead?

3. Flip it: name one decision where you currently give advice, or make a call, that someone else has to live with more than you do. What would change about how you decide if that weren't true?

Something to sit with

Taleb's asymmetry argument implies that "skin in the game" should be a filter you apply before trusting advice, not after it's already shaped your decision. Why is that filter so rarely applied in practice — is it that people don't think to ask, or that asking is socially awkward, or something else?

Talk it through with your tutor below. Your tutor is listening for whether you've actually traced the asymmetry — who gets the upside, who eats the downside — rather than just labeling someone untrustworthy.
