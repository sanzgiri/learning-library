The illusion of skill

Fooled by Randomness

Imagine ten thousand amateur traders, each with a coin. Every year, heads means their portfolio beats the market; tails means it doesn't. Pure chance. After five years, roughly three hundred of them will have flipped heads five times running. Business magazines will want to interview them.

Nobody flipping the coin has skill. But the survivors don't know that about themselves — and neither does anyone looking at them from outside. That's the trap this lesson is about.

The idea

A single win, or even a short streak of them, is weak evidence of skill. Survivorship bias is the reason: we only get to observe the winners. The thousands who tried the same approach and failed are invisible — they didn't write a book, get profiled, or found a company that's still around to ask. So every success we see is filtered through a process that already discarded most of the evidence that would have told us it was luck.

This is why Taleb's glossary calls out the lottery-ticket fallacy — treating a payoff as proof of a good strategy, when the strategy might just be "buy lots of tickets." The distinction that matters isn't "did it work" but "would it have worked most of the time, for most people who tried it, under different conditions?"

Going deeper: alternative histories

Taleb's sharpest tool for this is alternative histories — the other ways things could plausibly have gone, but didn't. His example: $10 million earned over a career of careful dentistry and $10 million earned from one successful round of Russian roulette spend exactly the same. But if you replayed each life a thousand times, the dentist's outcome barely varies, while most copies of the roulette player are dead. Same result, wildly different exposure to randomness — and that exposure, not the outcome, is what you should be judging.

The flip side is silent evidence: the alternative histories that didn't survive to be counted. A graveyard of failed traders who took the same risks as the survivor doesn't get interviewed. Every "how they did it" story you read has already been filtered by who happened to still be standing when the story got written.

Practice

Before you talk yourself into "luck vs. skill" being obvious: pick one real result you're tempted to credit to skill — yours or someone else's (a winning trade, a viral post, a hire that worked out). Work through all three:

1. If a hundred people had done roughly the same thing, how many of them would you expect to get the same result by chance alone?
2. What would it look like if you repeated the decision, not the outcome, a hundred times — i.e., what's the spread of alternative histories?
3. What's the "graveyard" here — who else made the same bet and isn't around to tell you about it?

Something to sit with

Taleb says you can't judge a decision by its outcome, only by the range of outcomes that were plausible when it was made. Push on that: if outcomes don't tell you who's skilled, what should you actually be evaluating people (including yourself) on — and how much harder is that to do than just checking who won?
