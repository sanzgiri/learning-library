LEVEL 10~12 MIN

Domain dependence

Antifragile

A surgeon who would never operate without checking and re-checking basic vitals will happily invest their savings on a hot tip from a friend. A skilled trader who instinctively understands convex payoffs will sign an inflexible lease with unlimited downside and no upside. Same person, same underlying pattern-recognition ability — but the skill doesn't travel.

The idea

Domain dependence is the failure to transfer an insight from the context where you learned it to a different context where it applies just as well. People "get it" in the classroom but not on the street, or in medicine but not in markets, or in their own trade but not in their personal finances. The old line about a hammer and nails is close but not quite right — domain dependence isn't about having only one tool, it's about having the right tool and simply not recognizing the job in front of you as the kind of job that tool is for.

This is worth taking seriously precisely because it's invisible from the inside. You don't experience domain dependence as ignorance — you experience total confidence in the domain where you're skilled, which makes it feel like your judgment should transfer everywhere. It doesn't announce itself.

Practice

1. Name one thing you're genuinely good at judging — a skill, a craft, a professional instinct. What is the underlying principle behind that judgment (not the specific skill, but the abstract pattern)?

2. Now find one domain of your life — money, relationships, health, a hobby — where that same abstract principle applies, but where you've been acting as if you didn't know it. What's different about how the domain "feels" that made the transfer not happen automatically?

Something to sit with

If domain dependence is invisible from the inside, how would you ever notice it in yourself without someone else pointing it out? Is there a way to audit your own judgment for this, or does it require an outside eye by definition?

Talk it through with your tutor below. Your tutor is listening for whether you've actually named the transferable principle, not just described two unrelated skills.
