LEVEL 17~10 MIN

Future blindness

Fooled by Randomness / Incerto Glossary

Someone with no theory of mind can't imagine that another person has beliefs, wants, and knowledge different from their own — the gap isn't a lack of intelligence, it's a missing faculty. Taleb argues we have a structurally similar gap, not toward other minds, but toward the future.

The idea

"Future blindness: our natural inability to take into account the properties of the future — like autism, which prevents one from taking into account the existence of the minds of others." Taleb, Incerto Glossary

The comparison is precise, not just colorful. Autism (in Taleb's analogy) isn't a deficit of general reasoning — someone can be sharp in every other way and still lack the specific faculty for modeling other minds. Future blindness works the same way: you can be brilliant, informed, and careful, and still be structurally unable to hold "the future will not resemble the past in the way I'm assuming" as a live possibility while planning. It's not that we forecast badly out of laziness — it's that imagining a genuinely different future takes a faculty most planning doesn't exercise at all.

This is the emotional, first-person cousin of the turkey problem — where that lesson is about the logic of induction failing, this one is about why the failure feels so natural from the inside. The turkey isn't stupid; it simply has no faculty for imagining Thanksgiving.

Practice

1. Pick one plan you're currently making (a career move, a financial decision, a project timeline) that implicitly assumes the near future will resemble the recent past. Try to genuinely imagine one specific way that assumption could be wrong — not "something unexpected could happen," but a concrete alternative future.

2. Was that exercise actually hard to do, or just unfamiliar? What's the difference between "I can't think of one" and "I have no faculty for this"?

Something to sit with

If future blindness is structural — not a knowledge gap you can close by reading more — what's the actual remedy? Is it building in insurance against being wrong (the barbell, again) rather than trying to see better, or is there something that genuinely improves the faculty itself?

Talk it through with your tutor below — they're listening for whether you found a genuinely different imagined future, not just a hedge like "who knows what could happen."
