The minority rule

Skin in the Game

Most soft drinks sold in the US are kosher-certified. Most airlines serve no peanuts. A large share of meat in the UK is halal. In every case, the group insisting on the constraint is a small minority — often 3-4% of the population. And yet the entire market bends to them, not the other way around.

The idea

Taleb's explanation is an asymmetry, not a headcount: a rigid minority that will never deviate is structurally stronger than a flexible majority that has no strong preference either way.

"A Kosher (or halal) eater will never eat nonkosher (or nonhalal) food, but a nonkosher eater isn't banned from eating kosher." Taleb, "The Most Intolerant Wins: The Dictatorship of the Small Minority"

Once that's true, the cheapest way to sell to everyone is to make one product that satisfies both groups — which means satisfying the minority's absolute constraint. Repeat this pattern across a supply chain, a market, or a culture, and a small, intransigent, uncompromising group ends up setting the default for the entire population, while everyone else barely notices they've been steered.

This connects straight back to skin in the game: the minority's rigidity usually comes from a real stake — a religious commitment, a health risk (an allergy), a moral line they won't cross. Their "skin in the game" is what makes them intransigent, and their intransigence is what makes them powerful, out of proportion to their numbers.

Practice

1. Find one example — in your workplace, your family, or a market you're familiar with — where a small, inflexible group's preference became the default for everyone. What made that group inflexible: a genuine stake, or just loudness? (The rule only works because of the former.)

2. Now find a place in your own life where you are the rigid minority of one — a rule you never break regardless of social pressure. What does holding that line cost you, and what does it get you?

Something to sit with

The minority rule can explain both admirable outcomes (a small group of principled holdouts improving a norm for everyone) and troubling ones (a small group imposing preferences nobody else asked for). What's the actual test for telling those two cases apart — is it about the content of the preference, the presence of real skin in the game, something else?

Talk it through with your tutor below. Your tutor is listening for whether you've identified the asymmetry mechanism itself, not just a list of examples.
