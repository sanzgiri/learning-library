The round-trip fallacy

The Black Swan / Incerto Glossary

"There's no evidence this bridge is unsafe" and "this bridge has been proven safe" sound similar. They are not the same claim, and treating them as interchangeable is how inspections get skipped and warnings get ignored — right up until the bridge that "had no evidence of being unsafe" fails.

The idea

Taleb names the swap directly.

"Round-trip fallacy: the confusion of absence of evidence of Black Swans (or something else) for evidence of absence of Black Swans (or something else). It affects statisticians and other people who have lost part of their reasoning by solving too many equations." Taleb, Incerto Glossary

The failure is directional and asymmetric, which is what makes it dangerous rather than just imprecise. "No evidence of X" is a statement about the limits of your search or your data. "Evidence that X doesn't exist" is a much stronger claim about the world. Going from the first to the second — the "round trip" — quietly converts "we haven't looked hard enough, or long enough, to see it" into "it isn't there," and the conversion tends to happen exactly in the domains (rare, high-impact events) where your data was least likely to have caught it in the first place. This is the turkey problem's twin from lesson 2: the turkey had no evidence of the axe, right up until it wasn't absent anymore.

Practice

1. Find one claim you've heard recently in the form "there's no evidence that X is a risk" (a food, a technology, a policy). Is that actually a claim that X was tested and cleared, or a claim that nobody has looked closely enough yet to know? How would you tell the difference from the outside?

2. Now find one place you've made this move yourself — treated "I haven't seen it go wrong" as "it won't go wrong." What would change if you treated those as two different claims requiring different evidence?

Something to sit with

You can't run every check forever — at some point "no evidence yet" has to be allowed to inform a decision, or nothing could ever be acted on. Where's the actual line between reasonable provisional trust and the round-trip fallacy? Is it about how much you've looked, how bad the downside is if you're wrong, or something else?

Talk it through with your tutor below. Your tutor is listening for whether you've distinguished "wasn't found" from "was ruled out" in your example, not just flagged something as uncertain.
